Theory of the Cochlea:

By Henry Vandyke Carter - Henry Gray (1918) Anatomy of the Human Body (See "Book" section below)Bartleby.com: Gray's Anatomy, Plate 920, Public Domain, https://commons.wikimedia.org/w/index.php?curid=566864

The cochlea is the auditory portion of the inner ear. It is a spiral-shaped cavity in the bony labyrinth, in humans making 2.5 turns around its axis, the modiolus. A core component of the cochlea is the Organ of Corti, the sensory organ of hearing, which is distributed along the partition separating fluid chambers in the coiled tapered tube of the cochlea.

A cool video showing the different important parts of the cochlea can be found here. We highly recommend that you watch this video if you are unfamiliar with the cochlea.

How to use this software

Once you load the data (from a data folder or the default data) and get to this window,

you have a few options:

  • Add in a waveform of the form f(t). This is the sound that is introduced into the cochlea. You can add in a combination of waveforms like sin(2*pi*2000*t)+sin(2*pi*4000*t). Keep the maximum frequency to less than 10 kHz. You can also experiment with sin(2*pi*1000*t^2) and other wave functions as you like. Then hit "See Spectrogram".
  • Add in voice. Due to some problems with different audio driver compatibility, the functionality has been temporarily turned off. Send me an email at asasmal@umich.edu if you would like to work with me on this.

Once you have the spectrogram, adjust the slider to change the fraction of time of the input that you would like to simulate on hte cochlea. Longer simulations take more time. I recommend you leave the slider at its 1/20th value to get reasonable speed. Then hit "Run Cochlear Simulation".

The next window will simulate the cochlear dynamics and neural dynamics. The different graphs are described below.

Acronyms:

BM: Basilar membrane.

disp.: Displacement.

The surface and contour plots on the left shows the BM normalised displacement at different positions along the cochlea and the normalised pressure on the BM respectively.

The neural activity plot shows the neural activation with time at different places along the cochlea. The neurons are excited by the BM motion but requires some time to get back to its "activate-able state". This time is called the refractory period.

The spectrogram at the bottom is the spectrogram of the input waveform. The green bar shows the current position inf time for the simulation.
